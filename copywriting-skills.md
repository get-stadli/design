# Stadli Copywriting

Write copy for **Stadli** — an all-in-one event intelligence platform built for modern sports franchises. Stadli unifies **ticketing, a 360° CRM, marketing campaigns, and real-time analytics** in one tool, with **AI woven throughout**. It helps teams sell more, fill seats, deepen fan relationships, and turn data into decisions.

The whole challenge of this skill lives in one sentence: **Stadli is a powerful B2B platform, but it must never sound like one.** The product is software; the voice is sport. Write about the full house, the fan you finally know by name, the night that mattered — not about dashboards, pipelines, or "unified solutions."

## The governing principle: sell the feeling, never the benefit

Look at what the inspiration actually sells. *"Longue vie aux tannants."* *"We The North."* *"Retomber en amour avec l'Aéroport."* *"On est là pour la vie."* Not one names a product — and, the part that's easy to miss, **not one names a benefit either.** They don't say "fly more comfortably," "save on tickets," or "we have loyal fans." They hand you a feeling and an identity and let you fall for it.

That bar is higher than "talk about the outcome instead of the feature." **"Sell more tickets," "fill more seats," "know your fans by name," "less admin" are benefits — and benefits are not the copy.** They're true, they're what Stadli does, and they belong on a comparison page, not in the voice. The voice lives one floor up: in what all of that *feels* like — the love between a city and its team, a building that fills on a cold Tuesday, the regular who's had the same seat for thirty years, the kid who just became a fan for life.

Stadli is the quiet thing behind that bond. Most of the time the product shouldn't appear at all; when it does, it's a whisper at the end, never the subject.

| What Stadli does (the benefit — keep it OUT of the copy) | What the copy reaches for (the feeling / identity) |
|---|---|
| Sells more tickets, fills seats | A full building. A city that showed up. The roar before the first whistle. |
| Fan data / 360° CRM | The fan you know by name. The regular in section 114. Thirty years, same seat. |
| Retention / win-back | Falling back in love with your team. The ones who never miss a night. |
| Analytics → decisions | Usually not worth saying out loud — it's plumbing, not feeling. |
| All-in-one platform / AI | The tool that disappears so the night can happen. |

The test: if a line states what Stadli *gives* you, it's a benefit — rewrite it until it's about what the night *feels* like. *"Sell more tickets"* becomes *"Longue vie aux partisans du mardi soir."*

## Say it the way you'd say it out loud — but make it feel something

There are two ways to miss, and the examples dodge both.

- **Cold cleverness** — reframes and wordplay that make the reader stop and decode (*"A season of noise. One clear call."*). Fails because nobody talks like that and it makes you work.
- **Flat benefit-speak** — *"Sell more tickets. Keep more fans."* Fails just as hard the other way: clear and spoken, but no soul, nothing to fall for.

The inspiration threads the needle — plain words, instantly clear, **and** full of warmth or wit: *"Pour les jeunes de 70 ans et plus."* *"Plus haut, plus vite, plus nous."* *"On s'en va-tu jouer dehors?"* You get each one immediately, they sound spoken, and they make you feel something or smile.

So plain is not the goal — *alive* is. Plain is just the constraint: land the feeling in words a real person would actually say. Three quick gates for every line:
- **Read-aloud:** would a person actually say this out loud? If it only works in print, it's too written.
- **First-read:** does it land instantly, no decoding? If you paused to "get it," so will they.
- **Feeling:** does it make you feel something or smile? If it only *informs*, it's flat benefit-speak — push it back up to the feeling.

## When you receive a request

1. **Identify the language.** Match the language of the request. If the user writes in French, deliver Québec French; in English, deliver English. If they ask for both or it's ambiguous and the surface is brand-facing, offer both. Never default to French-from-France.
2. **Identify the format** — slogan/headline, hero title, manifesto/long-form, social caption, sales email, pitch-deck line, CTA, microcopy. Slogans follow the punch register; prose follows the manifesto register (both defined below).
3. **Identify the surface and audience** — homepage, product page, sales deck, social, email, etc. The buyer is a franchise (team execs, ticketing/marketing directors); the emotional subject is usually the fan. See the surface guide below.
4. **Default to multiple options** for slogan/headline requests (typically 6–10). Keep the presentation clean — the lines should carry themselves. Close with 1–3 short, plain notes on the thinking behind your favorites (skip the device jargon unless the user is a copywriter who'd want it).

## Tone of voice

Three layers that coexist in every line:

- **Emotional and human** — the copy feels something. It reaches for the night, the crowd, the city that showed up on a Tuesday. It is never transactional first.
- **Confident and earned** — Stadli knows it's good, the way a serious team knows it's good. Quiet authority, not hype. No exclamation-point energy, no "revolutionary."
- **Clear and spoken** — short, plain sentences in the words people actually use out loud. The intelligence is in the idea and never in the vocabulary. If a line needs a clever turn or a SaaS noun to stand up, it isn't standing up.

The test: read it aloud. Would a real person say this to a colleague over coffee, *and* would a GM who's seen every pitch deck not roll their eyes? It has to pass both — plain enough to be spoken, sharp enough to be remembered. If it only works on paper, or if you had to read it twice, rewrite it.

## The two registers

Stadli's voice runs in two registers. Calibrate them separately — mixing them up is the most common mistake.

### 1. Punch (slogans, headlines, hero titles, CTAs, microcopy)

Short, plain, and easy to say. Keep it tight — usually **under 8 words** — but clarity always wins over the word count. Never compress a line to the point where the reader has to decode it. Punctuation does the work; periods create the natural beat of speech.

The bar: instantly clear, sounds spoken, and makes you feel something or smile. Sell the night and the fan, not what Stadli does.

Examples in voice (draft candidates, not yet client-approved):
- *Long live the Tuesday-night regulars.*
- *We don't fill seats. We fill them with people you'll remember.*
- *Every fan has a name. Act like it.*
- *There's no small night at the rink.*
- *Longue vie aux partisans du mardi soir.*
- *Faire le plein de souvenirs.*
- *Une salle pleine, c'est une ville qui dit oui.*
- *Il n'y a pas de petit soir de match.*
- *Trente ans plus tard, toujours le même siège. Ça, c'est de l'amour.*
- *Le plus beau bruit du monde, c'est une salle pleine.*

Notice none of them say "sell," "fill," or "know your fans" as a benefit — they put you in the building.

### 2. Manifesto (long-form, About copy, hero paragraphs, brand films, pitch openers)

Opens on a feeling or an identity — who these fans are, what a full building means, why a city keeps showing up — the way the inspiration manifestos do (*"On n'a pas choisi l'hiver. L'hiver nous a choisis."*). It is *not* a prose description of what the platform does. The product appears late and lightly, if at all — a whisper near the end. The landing line is everything; nothing should need a second read.

Example in voice (draft candidate, not yet client-approved):

> Some of them have had the same seat for thirty years. They were there for the cup, the collapse, the rebuild, and a hundred Tuesdays nobody else remembers. They don't show up for the standings. They show up because this team is theirs, and they decided that a long time ago. A franchise is just a building until those people walk in. Stadli helps you know every one of them by name — because they already know yours.

> Il y en a qui ont le même siège depuis trente ans. Ils étaient là pour la coupe, pour la dégringolade, pour la reconstruction, et pour cent mardis soirs que personne d'autre ne se rappelle. Ils ne viennent pas pour le classement. Ils viennent parce que cette équipe-là est la leur, et ça fait longtemps qu'ils l'ont décidé. Une foule comme celle-là, ça ne s'achète pas : ça se mérite, un soir à la fois. Stadli vous aide à connaître chacun d'eux par son prénom — parce qu'eux, ils vous connaissent déjà.

**The contrast between registers is the point.** A punchy headline over a measured manifesto creates the rhythm: the slogan catches, the prose convinces. If everything is punchy it reads as a caricature; if everything is measured it reads as corporate.

## Signature devices (seasoning, not the goal)

These devices show up across the inspiration. Use them **only when they make a line warmer or land the feeling faster** — never to sound clever for its own sake. A line that makes you feel something beats a clever one every time; a device that makes the reader stop and decode has failed, no matter how neat it looks. Wit is welcome when it's warm and instantly clear (*"Plus haut, plus vite, plus nous"*); it's out when it's cold or needs a second read. The inspiration also contains real, trademarked slogans from other brands (Apple, De Beers, etc.): learn the *device*, never reproduce or closely imitate a recognizable existing line as Stadli's own.

- **Contrast / turn** — set up, then flip, in plain words. *"Le droit est compliqué. Pas nous."*
- **Plain parallel** — two simple beats that mirror in structure. *"Plus fort, plus près."*
- **Warm reframe / wit** — redefine the thing or play on a word, but only if it lands instantly and makes you smile. *"Pour les jeunes de 70 ans et plus." / "Ton présent a de l'avenir."*
- **Sensory / moment opening** — start inside a felt, everyday moment before any product. The manifesto register lives here.
- **Collective voice** — "nous / on / we," team-and-fan as one. *"We The North."*
- **Product-as-enabler** — name the outcome, let the tool disappear.
- **Direct invitation** — warm, spoken imperative. *"Viens voir ça." / "Vivez-le en personne."*

A device is optional. Clarity and warmth are not.

## Language: Québec French + English

**The single biggest French mistake: writing English first and translating it.** A translated line keeps an English skeleton — the word order, the rhythm, the structure are all English, so it sounds foreign even when every word is correct. *"Connaissez vos partisans par leur nom"* is just "Know your fans by name" in disguise. Don't do this.

**Write French from scratch, as if English didn't exist.** Think the idea in Québec French and let it come out in a Québec shape. A good sign you've done it right: the French line *doesn't* translate cleanly back to English. *"Le match s'en vient. On s'occupe du reste."* and *"Savoir qui vient, savoir qui revient."* only work in French — that's why they sound native. The English and French versions of a Stadli idea should be cousins, not mirror images; never force them to match word-for-word.

**Reach for native Québec moves — warm, but clean:**
- **The "c'est ___" line** — the signature warm move. State the image, then name what it *is*. *Une salle pleine, c'est une ville qui dit oui. / Le plus beau bruit du monde, c'est une salle pleine. / Ça, c'est de l'amour.*
- **A real French idiom with a Stadli twist** — reach for an established expression, not a translated phrase. *Faire le plein de souvenirs* (from *faire le plein*) beats a literal "we fill memories." This is the instinct to develop.
- **"votre monde / ce monde-là"** — the warm word for your people/crowd, used naturally (not forced into every line).
- **rhyme and sound that only exists in French** — *qui vient, qui revient.* Free native texture.
- everyday concrete words: *le match, la partie, la salle, les gradins, l'aréna, le monde, le soir du match, le partisan.*

**Let the line breathe.** Don't over-clip into telegraphic fragments. *"Trente ans plus tard, toujours le même siège"* reads naturally; *"Trente ans, le même siège"* is choppy. Natural complete phrasing over clipped compression.

**Stay affirmative and warm.** Lead with the good image, not a cynical or empty one. *"Un aréna vide, c'est juste du béton"* fails — it opens on emptiness. Open on the full house, the love, the people who showed up.

**Calque traps to catch:**
- *"à une seule place"* for "in one place" → **au même endroit.**
- *"par leur nom"* tacked onto "connaître" → rephrase the whole idea.
- stiff literary imperatives **"sachez, soyez"** — nobody says these. Use *on*, a question, or a warmer verb.
- the English **"on ne fait pas X, on fait Y"** contrast — often a translated turn. Prefer a positive idiom instead.

**Register: warm but clean — NOT joual.** This is the key calibration. The voice is natural, spoken Québec French, but *soigné* — closer to clean conversational French than to tavern talk. Write **"il n'y a pas"**, not *"y'a pas"*. Avoid *pis, eux autres, la game, -tu* questions, and other heavy joual markers — even on social, keep them rare. The warmth comes from idioms, images, the *"c'est"* construction, and *on/nous* — never from dropping into joual.
- **Québec, not France.** Avoid *du coup, super, top, sympa, chouette.*
- **Tu / vous:** default **"vous"** for sales, product, and deck copy (you're addressing a franchise). Use **"on / nous"**, or **"tu"** only for clearly fan-voice lines, in the manifesto register. Never mix within one piece. *"On"* works as a warm subject in both registers.

**English** is contemporary North-American sports register — plain, confident, a little lean. Same rule in reverse: write it natively, don't translate from the French. No British tics, no corporate Americanisms.

## What NOT to sound like (the SaaS-cliché kill list)

This is the highest-risk failure mode for Stadli. Because the product *is* a platform, the easy words are all the wrong words. Strike on sight:

- *unlock, leverage, supercharge, empower, streamline, seamless, end-to-end, robust, scalable, holistic, synergy, ecosystem, solution, best-in-class, game-changing, next-level, revolutionary, cutting-edge, frictionless, turnkey, one-stop-shop.*
- *« solution clé en main, expérience client unique, propulsé par l'IA, leader incontournable, optimiser vos opérations »* and the rest of the French corporate kit.
- Feature-listing ("ticketing, CRM, analytics and more, all in one place!") — translate to outcome and feeling instead.
- Hype punctuation and intensifiers — *!!!, vraiment, incroyablement, ultra.*
- "AI-powered" as a bare badge. If AI earns a mention, frame it as quiet intelligence in service of a human result, never as the headline novelty.

The fast test: if the line would work, unchanged, for any other SaaS company, it has failed. Stadli copy must smell like a building full of people, not a feature page.

## Surface guide

- **Homepage hero** — the feeling, compressed. Product disappears. *Long live the Tuesday-night regulars.*
- **Product / feature pages** — the one place the benefit is allowed to surface, and even here lead with the feeling: an emotional headline, then *one* quiet, plain sentence that says what it does. "Vous" in French. Never a feature dump.
- **Manifesto / About / brand film** — the long register. Open on an identity or a feeling; earn the brand at the very end.
- **Sales deck openers** — a single line that makes the room feel the love of the game, not the ROI. The numbers come on the next slide.
- **Sales email** — warm, specific, short. One idea, one ask. No "I wanted to reach out."
- **Social** — conversational and warm, fan-energy. Still clean Québec French (no *la game / -tu* crutches); the looseness comes from a lighter, more playful tone, not from joual. Can run long but keep the snap.
- **CTAs** — verbs, 1–4 words. *Voir Stadli en action. Book a walkthrough.*

## Output format

For slogan / headline requests, keep it clean:

```
Brief contextual lead-in (1 sentence, optional).

1. Line one.
2. Line two.
…

Notes: 1–3 short, plain sentences on the thinking behind a couple of favorites.
```

Don't clutter each line with device labels or word counts unless the user asks for the analysis — the lines should stand on their own.

For manifesto / long-form: deliver the prose in the manifesto register (human-moment opening, product late and light, strong landing line). Follow with a 1–2 sentence note on the choice made — the moment you opened on, where the brand enters, the pronoun.

## Common pitfalls — self-check before answering

- **Sold a benefit instead of a feeling.** The number-one failure. *"Sell more tickets," "know your fans," "fill seats," "less admin"* are benefits — push every line up to what the night and the bond *feel* like. If it states what Stadli gives you, rewrite it.
- **Flat — plain but soulless.** Clear and spoken, but it doesn't make you feel anything or smile. Plain is the constraint, not the goal; *alive* is the goal.
- **Needed a second read (cold-clever).** The other side of the same coin. If the meaning didn't land instantly, it's too clever — rewrite it plainer, but don't flatten it into benefit-speak.
- **Doesn't sound spoken.** Read it aloud. If you wouldn't say it to a person, rework it.
- **The product showed up at all when it didn't need to.** Most lines shouldn't mention Stadli; when it appears, it's a whisper at the end.
- **Cloned an inspiration line.** Learn the spirit; the lines must be original to Stadli, not a lift of *Longue vie aux tannants* or any other source line.
- **Wrong register for the format.** A hero shouldn't read like body copy; a paragraph shouldn't read like a string of slogans.
- **French translated from English**, or **tu/vous slipped** within one piece, or drifted toward France.
- **Hype crept in.** No exclamation energy, no "revolutionary." The confidence is quiet.
- **It could be any SaaS.** Final gate: does it sound like a city that loves its team, or like a pricing page?
